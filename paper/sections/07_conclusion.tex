\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We ran the comparison the REST-API-testing literature has not: an LLM generator, a manual
EP/BVA suite, and EvoMaster, on the same three APIs, against a \emph{known} population of
\RQIIn{} seeded faults rather than a tally of errors observed on a live system.

The answer to ``can an LLM out-test a human baseline and a search-based tool'' is that the
question hides an equivocation. On every metric the field currently reports, the LLM wins:
\RQIcoveragePct\% endpoint coverage ($p=\RQIpvalue$), \RQIIItotalLlm{} edge-case scenarios
against \RQIIItotalManual{} ($p=\RQIIIpvalue$, rank-biserial $\RQIIIrankBiserial$),
\PDtestsLlm{} test cases to EvoMaster's \PDtestsEvo{}, and \PDerrTriggeredLlm{} of
\PDerrKey{} documented HTTP error behaviours triggered. On the ground truth it loses: Recall
\RQIIrecallLlm{} against EvoMaster's \RQIIrecallEvo{}, twice as many faults found by the
older, simpler tool ($p=\RQIImcnemarLEp$, underpowered at \RQIImcnemarLEpower{}, so a gap we
locate rather than establish).

The explanation is not about model capability. A generator that reads a specification writes
assertions about \emph{status codes}, and a fault that changes a computed value while leaving
the status intact is invisible to every such assertion, no matter how many are written.
EvoMaster's recorded-value regression oracles see exactly those faults. The evidence for this
mechanism is that the arms' ordering tracks fault character rather than generator
sophistication: EvoMaster leads on the arithmetic-heavy \texttt{rest-ncs}
(\RQIINcsEvo{} against \RQIINcsLlm{}), the LLM leads on the validation-heavy
\texttt{rest-scs} (\RQIIScsLlm{} against \RQIIScsEvo{}), and the LLM leads everywhere its
status oracle is adequate.

Three consequences follow. For practitioners, LLM generation is a breadth instrument that
complements rather than replaces value-based oracles --- our LLM would have missed half the
faults its intended replacement found. For the field, metrics that count what a suite
\emph{produces} ranked our arms in exactly the reverse of what they \emph{detect}, which
means the comparative claims currently in the literature are not measuring what their
framing implies. For benchmark design, fault populations should be stratified by
oracle-observability, because that single axis reorders the arms.

Our principal limitation is that the manual arm was not independently authored, which makes
the LLM-versus-manual fault comparison degenerate and uninterpretable
(Section~\ref{sec:threats}); we report it and decline to draw a conclusion from it. The
immediate future work is that repair --- an independently authored manual suite against the
same \RQIIn{} mutants, which our pipeline runs without modification. Beyond it, the natural
studies are an oracle-controlled comparison that fixes assertion kind and varies only the
generator, a fault population stratified by oracle-observability, and the GAP-T ablation
across model families. The pipeline, prompts, fault catalogs, and raw data are committed,
and every number above regenerates from them by script.
